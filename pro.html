Three ways in, one studio

The app. Your AI. Your terminal.

Fuchsia Studio Pro opens the same studio to three ways of working — and underneath all three sits one project: the same tape, the same clips, the same running order, whichever way you come in. Assemble by hand in the morning, ask your assistant to find something in the afternoon, let a script run overnight — it's one piece of work you keep coming back to, not three copies that drift apart.

In the app

The full studio

The complete Fuchsia Studio, exactly as it ships. Pro holds nothing back from the base app: it adds ways in, it doesn't take features out.

From your own AI · MCP

Put your assistant to work

Connect Studio to the AI assistant app you already use — whichever provider you prefer — and put it to work on your tape: find the moments, propose the cuts, answer “what's actually on this recording?” The grounding holds no matter who's asking — what's said about your tape is quoted verbatim from it, or refused.

From the command line · CLI

Script it and batch it

Script the parts of the work that repeat, and batch them to run unattended. Finish the same way: export a finished, loudness-targeted master — 24-bit WAV or AAC — with no app window open. Headless, repeatable, CI-friendly. If your tape came in at mixed sample rates, it masters transparently anyway.

Still honest. Still yours.

Automation doesn't mean handing over the wheel.

Everything that makes Studio trustworthy holds in Pro, whichever surface is doing the work. When the AI works, it works on your own key and your own account, sending only text — never your raw audio. The grounding travels with the tape, not the interface: what's said about your recordings is quoted verbatim or refused, whether the question comes from the app, your assistant, or a script. And Pro adds a guarantee of its own — every automated change is attributed, so when a script or an assistant touches your program, the change carries its origin and you always know which hands did what.

“You're still the author. You just have more hands now — and every one of them signs its work.”
