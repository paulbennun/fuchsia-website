How it works

How Fuchsia Studio works.

Here’s the whole path, from a folder of raw interviews to a broadcast-ready master. It’s shorter than you’d think, and almost all of it happens in the transcript — you work from what people actually said.

The walkthrough

From a folder of tape to one master.

Ten steps for the craft, plus one optional step for people who script their work. Almost all of it lives in the transcript.

1

Install and open — no account

Download Fuchsia Studio from the Mac App Store and open it. That’s the whole setup: no sign-up, no login, no cloud project to create. You land in a native Mac document window and open an episode — or start one. Studio is $129, one time; it runs on macOS 26 on an Apple silicon Mac.

2

Add your tape — at any sample rate

Drag in your interviews and field recordings — hours of them, at whatever sample rates your sessions produced. Studio transcribes everything and separates the speakers, and both jobs run entirely on your Mac: transcription uses Apple’s on-device speech engine, and speaker separation happens locally too. Your audio doesn’t go anywhere to be understood.

Speakers arrive as unnamed voices — Speaker 1, Speaker 2 — and you can rename them as you recognize who’s who. (If you later opt in to the AI features, Studio can propose names — but only when someone actually says the name on tape. More on that in step 8.)

3

Read the tape, then search it

Open Tape view (⌘3) and your source lays out as a readable page — the interview as text, verbatim, with every word still anchored to its moment in the audio. Press ⌘F and type a keyword to land on a half-remembered line across hours of material.

And when a moment strikes you, select it: Studio surfaces the passages elsewhere in your tape that echo it, even when they’re worded differently. Both ways of finding things — the keyword search and the related moments — run on your Mac, with no key, no account, and no network call. To be precise about what each does: the search box finds the words people said; the echoes start from a moment you select, not from a typed theme.

4

Select the words — that’s a clip

This is the gesture the whole app is built around. Drag across the words that matter in the transcript, and your selection lifts into a clip. You build the piece from what people said, instead of scrubbing a waveform hunting for a line you half-remember.

5

Shape a running order

Switch to Assemble (⌘1) and put your clips in order. This is your piece as structure — one running order you sculpt scene by scene, not a multitrack timeline. When a cut lands mid-word, it snaps clean, so you never lose a syllable. Whenever you want to check a clip against its source, Show in Tape (⌘J) jumps straight to its place in the original interview. And to read the whole episode as one continuous document, there’s Script view (⌘2).

6

Work a clip like tape

Open any clip to refine it. Scrub under the loupe with varispeed tape-rock — it behaves like rocking reels on a deck. Trim to the word. Cuts snap to the natural silences in the speech, and you can override the snap whenever your ear disagrees.

Where a join needs a breath, add authored air: a beat of quiet that belongs to the story. Air is a stored, visible value on the seam — you can always see it, adjust it, or clear it. It’s never a hidden default.

7

Record narration where it plays

Write your narration at the exact point in the piece where it will play, and record takes right there. Every take becomes tape — transcribed like the rest of your material, so your own voice is as searchable and cuttable as your interviews.

One thing Studio deliberately does not do: make a voice. There’s no synthesis, no cloning, no machine narration. Narration is always your own recorded performance, through your own microphone.

8

Optional: connect an AI provider

Everything above runs with no AI at all. If you want it, connect your own AI provider — your key, your account — and Studio adds the reads: the Dramaturg, a grounded collaborator that finds moments, proposes cuts, and answers “what is this section really about?”; interpretations of your material that land in Reading view (⌘4); and those speaker-name proposals — a name is suggested only when someone says it on tape, the moment plays for you, and you confirm. Names you’ve typed yourself are never overwritten.

The honest shape of this: it’s opt-in, it runs on your own provider account rather than on your Mac, and Studio sends only text — never your raw audio. Connecting doesn’t sweep your existing archive; it’s the new tape you add — your own or licensed — that gets read automatically, and you can skip any single import or pause a whole library. And in every case the AI proposes while you approve: it quotes the tape verbatim or it refuses — it never invents a line, never misattributes a speaker, and never drafts your narration.

9

Repair wounded tape

Real-world tape gets hurt, so Studio ships two repairs, both deterministic. De-hum notches out mains hum and its harmonics (50 or 60 Hz). Declip rebuilds peaks that were clipped at recording. Each one subtracts what shouldn’t be there — neither ever invents audio that wasn’t recorded. Like the rest of the craft, they run on your Mac.

10

Finish and export one master

When the piece is cut, bring it to broadcast loudness with a finishing stage built for the spoken word, then export a single loudness-targeted master — 24-bit WAV or AAC — that drops cleanly into Logic, Ableton, Audition, or wherever your delivery lives. Sample rate takes care of itself: bring it all in at any rate, and a source already at the master rate passes through untouched — byte for byte; anything else is converted with mastering-grade resampling. Studio masters to the broadcast-standard 48 kHz.

To be straight about scope: v1.0 exports that one mastered file. Stems, session export, and AAF/OMF interchange are on the roadmap, not in the app today.

11

Optional: drive it headless with Studio Pro

If your work is pipelines, Studio Pro adds a command line and an MCP connection, so you can drive the same studio from your own AI assistant: batch a week of tape overnight, then render a finished, loudness-targeted master with no app window open. Every automated change is attributed, so you can always see exactly what a script or an assistant did to your program.

Pro is a separate subscription, bought direct from Fuchsia rather than the App Store — and nothing in the base app is locked behind it. Pro adds automation; the whole craft above is yours for the one-time price.

That’s the path: tape in, read it, select the words, shape the order, give the seams air, speak your narration, finish to loudness, and hand one master to the world. The core craft — import to master — happens on your own machine, and you stay the author of every cut.

What Studio can and can’t do

Plain answers about the edges — what’s in the app today, and what isn’t.

How does search work in Studio?

Studio gives you two ways to find things, and it’s worth being exact about what each one does. The search box is keyword search: press ⌘F, type a word or phrase, and Studio finds where it was actually said — verbatim, across hours of tape. Related moments is the second path: select a passage that matters and Studio surfaces the places in your tape that echo it, even when the words are different. Both run entirely on your Mac — no key, no account, no network connection.

What Studio doesn’t have is a box where you type a theme — “grief”, “leaving home” — and get back the concept. Finding by idea starts from a moment you select, not from free text. And naming the themes and through-lines in your tape is the job of the optional AI reads, which run on your own provider account once you choose to connect one.

Does it identify who’s speaking?

It separates them; it doesn’t recognize them. When you add tape, Studio automatically tells the voices apart — that happens on your Mac, no account — and each speaker arrives unnamed: Speaker 1, Speaker 2, and so on. You can name a voice yourself at any point, and a name you type is never overwritten.

If you’ve connected an AI provider (optional, on your own key), Studio can also propose a name — but only when someone actually says it on tape. The proposal arrives with the moment attached: you play it, hear the evidence, and confirm. No confirmation, no name.

Can it create or clone a voice?

No. There’s no voice synthesis in Studio — nothing that generates, imitates, or clones a voice. Narration is your own performance: you write it at the point in the piece where it plays, record takes right there through your microphone, and every take becomes tape, transcribed like the rest of your material. What your listeners hear is what you recorded.

Does the AI write or decide for me?

No. It proposes; you decide. And it’s off until you turn it on — the AI features are opt-in, connected through your own provider account, and Studio sends them text only, never your audio.

Once connected, the Dramaturg behaves like a well-prepared collaborator: it finds moments, proposes cuts, and answers questions like “what is this section really about?” When it speaks about your tape, it quotes it verbatim — or it refuses. It doesn’t draft your narration, invent a line, or put words in the wrong mouth. And nothing it proposes changes your story until you approve it. You stay the author.

Can I export a session, stems, or an AAF?

Not in v1.0. Studio exports one finished master — a loudness-targeted, 24-bit WAV or AAC — from a finishing stage built for the spoken word. That file drops cleanly into Logic, Ableton, Audition, or wherever your work goes next, and mixed-rate sessions master transparently.

Session interchange — stems, AAF/OMF, the parts rather than the whole — is on the roadmap. If your workflow depends on handing a session to a mix engineer, Studio isn’t there yet: today it hands over the finished piece.

Search & finding moments

Two ways to find things — and both run on your Mac, with the network off.

Does search need an AI key, an account, or a network connection?

No — none of the three. Both the keyword search box and the related-moments feature run on an index built on your Mac, and neither makes a network call. Search works with the network off.

Can Studio name the themes and through-lines in my tape?

Not from search itself. Search finds words (keyword) and echoes a moment you select (related moments); it doesn’t hand you a named theme. Naming the themes and through-lines is interpretive work, and that’s the job of the optional AI reads — which run on your own provider account, only once you choose to connect one.

Your tape & your privacy

The category we expect you to read most closely, so the answers are scoped precisely. The short version: the craft runs on your Mac, your audio never leaves it, and the AI is something you turn on — on your own account — not something that happens to you.

Does my audio ever leave my Mac?

No. Your audio stays on your Mac — through transcription, speaker separation, search, editing, assembly, and finishing. That holds even if you later connect an AI provider: the Dramaturg and the reads work from text, and Studio sends text only, never your recordings.

Exactly what runs on my machine, and what doesn’t?

On your Mac, with no account and no network connection: import at any sample rate, transcription (Apple’s on-device speech engine), speaker separation, both search paths and the index behind them, clip editing and assembly, narration recording, the two tape repairs (de-hum and declip), and finishing to a loudness-targeted master.

Not on your Mac: the optional AI reads — the Dramaturg, the interpretations that land in Reading view (⌘4), and speaker-name proposals. Those run on a hosted AI provider you connect yourself, on your own key and account, and they receive text only. If you never connect a provider, that whole layer simply stays off.

Do I have to upload my tape to a cloud to transcribe, search, or edit it?

No. There is no upload, no cloud project, and no account. Studio transcribes, separates speakers, indexes, and edits entirely on your Mac — you can do the whole craft with the network off.

When I connect an AI provider, what actually gets sent — my audio, or just text?

Just text. Everything in the AI layer — the Dramaturg, the interpretations in Reading view, the speaker-name proposals — works from text drawn from your tape, so what leaves your Mac is text, sent to the provider you connected under your own account. Your audio files are never sent.

Whose AI account and API key are used — mine or Fuchsia’s?

Yours. AI is opt-in, and turning it on means connecting your own provider account with your own key. There is no Fuchsia account behind it — your agreement is with the provider you chose, and any AI usage costs are between you and them.

If I turn on AI, does Studio upload my entire existing archive?

No. Connecting a provider doesn’t sweep the tape you already have — nothing from your existing archive is sent on its own. What gets read automatically is new tape you add from then on — your own or licensed material — and even there you stay in charge: you can skip the read for any individual import, and pause reading for an entire library.

One thing to know precisely: if you later ask the Dramaturg about older material, or open a read on it, the text needed to answer is sent at that moment — because you asked, not because Studio swept.

Can I keep specific recordings from ever being sent to the AI?

Yes. If you never connect a provider, nothing is sent at all. If you do connect one, the automatic read is yours to control: skip it for any individual import, or pause it for a whole library. Beyond the automatic read, text is sent only when you yourself ask the Dramaturg or open a read on material — those requests happen when you make them, not on a schedule.

What about material I don’t have the right to share?

Studio doesn’t police this for you, and we think that’s the right call — we can’t know what permissions you hold, and inherited tape often comes with a waiver that covers you. You decide what gets sent. The controls are yours: don’t connect a provider at all and nothing is ever sent; skip the automatic read on any single import; or pause it for a whole library. And in every configuration, only text is ever sent — never your audio.

Is the search index built on my machine or in a cloud?

On your machine. Both retrieval paths — keyword search across your tape, and the related moments that echo a passage you select — run against an index built on your Mac. No key, no account, no network call, for the index or the searches.

I do investigative work with confidential sources — is this safe for that?

We designed the boundary to be exact so you can make that judgment with full knowledge. Out of the box, Studio makes no network calls for the craft and asks for no account: your tape, transcripts, search index, and edits live on your Mac, and your audio never leaves in any configuration.

If you choose to connect an AI provider, the AI layer works by sending text — never audio — to that provider, on your key, under your agreement with them. That text moves on two paths, and you should know both. First, new tape you add (your own or licensed) is read automatically, and you can skip any import or pause a whole library. Second, when you yourself ask the Dramaturg a question or open a read — including on tape that was in your archive before you connected — the text needed to answer is sent at that moment.

Whether a given provider is appropriate for a given source is your call, as it should be. Studio’s job is to make sure you always know exactly what would leave, and to keep everything else on your machine.

The AI reads & the Dramaturg

Optional, opt-in, and grounded in your tape — here’s exactly how the reads behave.

What are the AI reads, and what is the Dramaturg?

They’re Studio’s optional layer of interpretation. Connect your own AI provider and Studio can read new tape as you add it, landing its interpretations in Reading view (⌘4) as material you can draw on — and it gives you the Dramaturg, a collaborator you can ask to find moments in your source, propose cuts, and answer “what is this section really about?” The role is deliberately bounded: the AI reads and proposes. It never writes your story, and it never changes anything without you.

Do I need them to use Studio?

No. Studio is a complete tool without them. Importing, transcription, speaker separation, search, editing, assembly, and finishing all run on your Mac with no account, no key, and no network. If you never connect an AI provider, you’re not using a lesser version of the app — you’re using the whole craft.

What can the Dramaturg actually do?

Three things: find moments in your tape, propose cuts, and answer questions about what your material is saying. It works from your transcripts, and it holds to one working rule — when it refers to your material, it quotes the tape verbatim, or it refuses. It proposes; it never decides. Nothing it suggests touches your program until you approve it.

Does the AI make edits on its own, or do I approve every change?

You approve every change. Anything the Dramaturg proposes — a cut, a placement — arrives as a proposal showing you plainly what it refers to, and nothing moves until you say yes. You stay the author; the AI is a reader in the room.

Will the AI ever put words in my subject’s mouth, invent a quote, or misattribute a speaker?

No. This is the Dramaturg’s working rule, not a best effort: it quotes the tape verbatim or it refuses. It never invents a line, never drafts words for anyone, and never attributes speech to the wrong voice. In documentary work a misattributed quote is a serious failure, and Studio is built as if that’s true.

How are the AI’s answers about my tape grounded — can I trust them?

Every answer and every proposal is tied back to the actual material it cites, so you check it against the tape itself — not against the AI’s say-so. And honestly: it can still point at the wrong moment. That’s exactly why nothing it proposes takes effect until you’ve seen what it resolved to and approved it. Trust here comes from the method, not from a promise that the model is infallible.

Does the AI write or draft my narration for me?

No. The Dramaturg finds, proposes, and answers questions — it doesn’t draft. Narration in Studio is your own work: you write it at the point in the piece where it plays and record it in your own voice, and every take becomes tape like the rest of your material.

How does the speaker-name proposal work, and can it get a name wrong?

Speakers arrive unnamed — Speaker 1, Speaker 2 — and stay that way unless you name them or opt in to the reads. With AI connected, Studio proposes a name only when someone actually says it on tape: the moment plays, and you confirm before the name is applied. Could a proposal be wrong? Yes — tape is messy, and that’s exactly why the moment plays and you decide. A name you’ve typed yourself is never overwritten.

Is the AI on by default, or do I have to turn it on?

It’s off. Out of the box Studio has no AI connected and sends nothing anywhere — there’s nothing to opt out of. The reads switch on only when you connect your own provider account. From then on, new tape you add — your own or licensed material — is read automatically so the interpretations are ready when you are; you can skip the read for any individual import, and pause a whole library at any time. Connecting doesn’t reach back and sweep the archive you already have.

Which AI providers can I connect?

Studio supports multiple AI providers at launch, so you connect your own account with the provider you already use, on your own API key — the reads and the Dramaturg run on that provider’s models, under your account, and they only ever receive text. There’s no local-model or self-hosted path in v1.0: the reads are a cloud workload by design, which is part of why they’re strictly opt-in.

What does the AI cost, and how do the keys work?

Studio itself is a one-time $129 purchase. The reads run on your own provider account, under your own API key, so your provider bills you directly at their rates — what the reads cost depends on how much tape you have them read, and that’s between you and your provider. If you never connect a provider, the whole craft still works, and there’s no usage to be billed for.

Can the AI reads run offline?

No. The reads and the Dramaturg are a cloud workload: they run on your provider’s models, over the network, on your own key — there’s no local-model mode. Everything else in Studio works with no connection at all: import, transcription, speaker separation, search, editing, assembly, and finishing are all on-device.

Studio Pro

What does Studio Pro add on top of the base app?

Two things: a command line, and an MCP server you can connect to your own AI assistant. That’s the entire difference — Pro is the same studio with more ways to drive it. Connect it and your own assistant can work in your Studio projects: find moments in the tape, propose cuts, answer questions about the material, and render a finished master. When it’s done, open the piece in Studio and carry on by hand — every change the assistant made is attributed, so you can see exactly what it did.

Is anything in the base app locked behind Pro?

No — nothing. The base app is the whole craft: import, transcription, speaker separation, search, clips, assembly, narration, tape repair, finishing and export all ship in Studio itself, complete. Pro doesn’t unlock features; it adds ways to drive the ones that are already there.

Where do I buy Pro, and is it a subscription?

Studio Pro is sold directly by Fuchsia at fuchsiaprojects.com, not through the Mac App Store, and it’s a subscription. The base app is separate and stays a one-time $129 purchase on the Mac App Store — nothing in it ever needs Pro.

Finishing & export

One programme, one master — here’s exactly what comes out of Studio.

How do I finish to broadcast loudness?

When the piece is ready, Studio masters it on your Mac — no account, no upload. The render measures loudness to the broadcast standard (ITU-R BS.1770), brings the whole piece to your chosen target, holds the peaks with a true-peak limiter, and dithers the file on the way out. It’s a finishing stage built for the spoken word, and it treats your piece as one programme: a single measured landing for the whole thing, not a clip-by-clip re-balance. After the bake, Studio shows you the measured loudness — before and after, as plain fact — and offers an A/B against your rough cut, so you hear exactly what finishing did before you deliver.

What can I export from Studio?

One finished master: a single loudness-targeted audio file of your whole piece. Export works like a print dialog — File ▸ Export… (⌘E) — where you choose the format and the loudness target, confirm, and Studio bakes the file. Before it renders, a readiness check names anything that isn’t ready — an offline clip, for instance — so you never discover a hole after delivery.

What file formats and loudness targets does export support?

Two formats: 24-bit WAV, the lossless file for handing to an editor or a DAW, and AAC (.m4a), the compressed file podcast hosts ingest directly. The loudness target is chosen per export, right in the sheet; the default is the common podcast standard, −16 LUFS. There’s no MP3 export in v1.0 — macOS has no built-in MP3 encoder and Studio doesn’t bundle one — but between WAV and AAC, both the handoff and the publish are covered.

Will my finished master drop cleanly into Logic, Ableton, or Audition?

Yes. The master is a standard audio file with nothing proprietary about it — the 24-bit WAV drops straight into Logic, Ableton, Audition, or wherever you finish. If Studio’s master is your final deliverable, ship it; if the piece goes on to a final mix elsewhere, hand the WAV to that session.

Can I export stems, a session, or an AAF/OMF to keep working in my DAW?

Not in v1.0. Studio exports one mastered file — no stems, no session export, no AAF/OMF. Session and stem interchange is on the roadmap. The handoff that works today is the 24-bit WAV master: it carries the whole finished piece into any DAW.

What happens with mixed-rate tape when I export?

Sample-rate worries are a thing of the past. Cut a 44.1 kHz archive clip against 48 kHz field tape and never think about it — just bring it all in. Studio masters to the broadcast-standard 48 kHz: a source already at that rate passes through untouched, byte for byte, and anything at a different rate is converted with mastering-grade resampling. The export report names what changed — say, 44.1 → 48 kHz — so you always know exactly what happened.

Can I round-trip a project back into Studio from another tool?

No — export is one-way: a finished master out. But your project never leaves Studio in the first place. The episode stays fully editable on your Mac, so when the piece changes you re-export a fresh master rather than round-tripping. Project interchange with other tools is on the roadmap, alongside session and stem export.
